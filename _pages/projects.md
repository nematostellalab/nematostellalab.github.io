## Our Research

The Leach Lab studies behavior, circadian biology, and host–environment interactions in the starlet sea anemone, *Nematostella vectensis*. We combine low-cost technology, experimental biology, and student-driven research to explore how organisms interact with their environment across time.

---

## Building Accessible Behavioral Imaging Systems

We are developing a **low-cost, modular imaging platform** to study behavior in *Nematostella vectensis*. Using a Raspberry Pi and custom lighting system, this setup allows continuous recording across light–dark cycles without disrupting natural rhythms.

Our goal is to make behavioral research **more accessible**, especially in undergraduate teaching labs, by removing the need for expensive imaging equipment.

---

## Circadian Behavior and Sex Differences

This project explores how **male and female anemones behave differently across the day-night cycle**.

Students measure:
- Tentacle extension and retraction  
- Body length changes  
- Whole-organism movement  

By tracking these behaviors over time, we ask:
Do males and females show different circadian patterns?

This is a highly integrative project that gives hands-on experience with experimental design, animal behavior, data analysis, robotics, and coding.

---

## Mucus, Microbes, and Marine Defense

What’s in an anemone’s slime?

This project investigates whether *Nematostella vectensis* produces **antimicrobial peptides (AMPs)** in its mucus. These molecules may help defend against bacteria, fungi, and even viruses.

We hope to test different microbial systems (bacteria, fungi, protozoa, viruses), building a broader picture of how mucus functions as a **biological defense layer**.

---

## Temperature and Sex Determination

How does sex get determined in *Nematostella vectensis*?

This project tests whether **temperature influences sex ratios** during development. By raising animals at different temperatures, we can ask:

Does the environment shape whether an anemone becomes male or female?

This work connects development, environment, and reproductive biology.
